Social Art and Healthcare Metaphors

February 28 2010, 10:41 AM  by Brian Condon

To the wonderful Ontario College of Art and Design for a seminar by Natalie Jeremijenko hosted by Greg van Alynstyne and his colleagues at the sLab of the OCAD (http://slab.ocad.ca/).

The themes that emerged for me were about co-creation, collaboration and creativity. And the use of a well-known metaphor - that of healthcare, to explore how the 'health' of a neighbourhood can be improved by "Impatients" - who are unwilling to wait for 'the Man' to intervene through legislation. The use of the healthcare metaphor is interesting and, as participants pointed out, as well as the positive aspects of caring, nurturing, curing and well-being; there are also some potentially negative aspects related to the power relationships between clinicians and patients, the financially burdensome nature (some said 'broken' nature) of healthcare in developed countries and the fact that healthcare needs so much in terms of resources (both human and natural). Having said all that, treated carefully and not subject to everyone going into 'metaphor overdrive' - it's a helpful framework for thinking and is being used successfully in New York.

    Natalie Jeremijenko is an artist whose background includes studies in biochemistry, physics, neuroscience and precision engineering. Jeremijenko is a recipient of the 2008-2009 Van Alen Institute-New York Prize Fellowship in Sustainable Cities and the Social Sciences, and was recently named one of the 40 most influential designers by I.D. Magazine. She is an artist not-in-residence at the Institute for the Future (IFTF) in Palo Alto. Jeremijenko directs the xDesign Environmental Health Clinic [http://www.nyu.edu/projects/xdesign/] at NYU. Her work is described as experimental design, hence xDesign, as it explores opportunities presented by new technologies for non-violent social change. Her research centers on structures of participation in the production of knowledge and information, and the political and social possibilities (and limitations) of information and emerging technologies — mostly through public experiments.

An important aspect is that the mice are geographically limited - and so effects are locally verifiable.
